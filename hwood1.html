Part I

Scattered Actualities & Scenics

1895 – 1906

1895 – 1897

Motion pictures debut commercially in the United States. Production is concentrated in major urban centers, especially New York City and Chicago. Early filmmaking depends on proximity to theatrical talent, equipment manufacturers, distribution exchanges, and urban audiences. Southern California is absent from trade-press discussions of production centers.

1898 – 1902

Cameramen from companies such as the Edison Manufacturing Company and the Biograph Company travel west occasionally to capture short "actualities" and scenic footage. Subjects include Pasadena parades, orange groves, and harbor scenes in Los Angeles. These films are non-narrative and produced by itinerant cameramen.

No production infrastructure exists in Southern California. The industry's geographic center remains the New York region, particularly Fort Lee, New Jersey.

1903 – 1906

Occasional scenic footage continues in California, but narrative filmmaking remains Eastern-based. Fort Lee becomes the dominant early production hub because of its proximity to New York studios, inexpensive land, and dramatic Palisades landscapes. Southern California remains geographically peripheral to the industry.

Part II

The Turn West

1907 – 1909  ·  Narrative Production Begins

1907

Francis Boggs of the Selig Polyscope Company begins narrative production in Los Angeles. Early filming reportedly occurs on a rooftop studio near Main Street and 8th Street in downtown Los Angeles. His goal is pragmatic rather than visionary: to exploit year-round outdoor filming conditions.

This represents the first sustained narrative production unit operating in the Los Angeles region — the first real test of California as a film-production base.

1908

The feature adaptation The Count of Monte Cristo is filmed partly in Southern California. Locations likely include Santa Catalina Island, Pasadena foothills, and Los Angeles coastal areas. These productions demonstrate the visual potential of California landscapes. However, Hollywood's dominance is far from inevitable.

Simultaneous Development Elsewhere

Jacksonville emerges as a winter filmmaking hub. Companies such as the Kalem Company begin producing films there. Trade-journal commentary in The Moving Picture World offered sustained coverage of Jacksonville's growing operations across multiple volumes:

"The Kalem Company's Florida unit continues to produce excellent subjects under ideal conditions in Jacksonville, with mild weather allowing uninterrupted exteriors impossible in the North. Beaches, rivers, and historic sites provide authentic settings for marine dramas and Southern stories, while the stock company delivers high-quality one-reelers at a rapid pace."

The Moving Picture World, Vol. 9  ·  July–September 1911  —  archive.org/details/moviwor09chal

"The Kalem Company has been receiving many flattering testimonials from exhibitors regarding the quality of their Florida-made subjects. One enthusiastic convert from Jacksonville, Fla., writes: 'Your Florida pictures are the best I have seen — authentic Southern backgrounds and perfect weather make them stand out.'"

The Moving Picture World, Vol. 10  ·  October–December 1911  —  archive.org/details/moviwor10chal  ·  Search: "Kalem" + "Jacksonville"

These statements show that Florida was actively promoted as a rival production center. Notably, both volumes appeared during the same months that Richard V. Spencer was publishing his predictions about Los Angeles — suggesting the trade press was simultaneously boosting both cities as viable production destinations.

Part III

Permanent Footing

1909 – 1911  ·  Infrastructure Emerges

1909

The Selig Polyscope Company establishes a more permanent facility in downtown Los Angeles at the Sing Kee Chinese Laundry lot on Olive Street between 7th and 8th Streets. This marks a transition from itinerant filmmaking to fixed studio infrastructure.

1910

Production activity expands rapidly across Los Angeles. Important early facilities include:

  • Edendale — Selig opens a larger studio there.
  • A makeshift studio used by D. W. Griffith of the Biograph Company operates inside a Pacific Electric trolley yard in downtown Los Angeles.
  • Bison Film Company establishes facilities at 1712 Allesandro Street.

The Edendale Production Colony (1910s)

During the early 1910s, Edendale becomes the first true film colony in Los Angeles. Companies operating there include Keystone Studios and Pathé Frères, with key figures including Mack Sennett and Charlie Chaplin. At Keystone, Chaplin creates the iconic character The Tramp.

1911 — Trade-Press Recognition

By 1911, industry observers begin publicly recognizing Los Angeles's potential. In April 1911, The Moving Picture World published an article titled "Los Angeles as a Producing Center" by trade writer Richard V. Spencer. Spencer described Southern California's advantages: mountains shielding the region from storms, nearby beaches, varied landscapes, and abundant sunlight. He estimated roughly 320 workable filming days per year.

"By reason of the advantages set forth, it is predicted that Los Angeles will become known to the world not as the second largest picture producing center, but the largest, bar none."

Richard V. Spencer  ·  The Moving Picture World, April 1911

At the time, this was speculative rather than consensus.

October 27, 1911

The Nestor Film Company opens the first permanent studio in Hollywood at Sunset Boulevard and Gower Street. Hollywood was chosen because of inexpensive land, agricultural zoning, rail access, and fewer municipal restrictions than downtown Los Angeles.

December 1913

Cecil B. DeMille and Jesse L. Lasky establish a studio inside a rented barn at Selma Avenue and Vine Street. This site later becomes known as the Lasky-DeMille Barn.

Part IV

The Patent Trust Factor

1908 – 1912

1908 – 1912

The formation of the Motion Picture Patents Company reshapes film-industry geography. The MPPC aggressively enforces patent control over cameras and film equipment in Eastern production centers.

Southern California offers:

  • Geographic distance from enforcement agents
  • Large open spaces for filming
  • Reliable weather for year-round production

These factors encourage independent producers to relocate westward.

Part V

The Competition Years

1908 – 1915

1908 – 1915

During this period the American film industry remains geographically fluid. Competing production centers include Jacksonville, San Antonio, New Orleans, and Phoenix. Jacksonville becomes the most significant rival.

By the mid-1910s roughly 25–30 studios operate there seasonally, and the city is sometimes described as the "Winter Film Capital."

However, Florida production faces structural limits:

  • Seasonal operations
  • Limited terrain diversity
  • Civic resistance to film crews
Part VI

Industrial Lock-In

1912 – 1915

1912 – 1915

Between 1912 and 1915 the industry consolidates in Southern California. Reinforcing forces include accelerating studio construction, clustering of labor and suppliers, real-estate development supporting studios, and increasing demand for consistent outdoor locations for feature films.

Once this agglomeration economy forms, relocation becomes increasingly difficult. By 1915, Los Angeles becomes the dominant American film-production center.

Key Insights
  • Before 1905, Southern California was not seriously considered as a production base.
  • Between 1907 and 1912, multiple cities competed to host the industry.
  • Jacksonville and Los Angeles were both promoted in trade journals as ideal filming environments — often in the same volumes of The Moving Picture World, published within weeks of each other.
  • Hollywood's eventual dominance resulted from climate advantages combined with industrial clustering, not from an initial strategic decision.
