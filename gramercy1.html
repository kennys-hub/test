1746 North Gramercy Place
A History of a Hollywood Property

Hollywood, California  ·  c. 1888 – Present

The property at 1746 North Gramercy Place tells the story of Hollywood itself — from semi-rural orchard land to a quiet middle-class suburb, through the golden age of the film capital, and into the postwar urban density that reshaped the neighborhood.

Chapter I

The Lick Estate & Busseyer Farmland

Pre-1888 – 1905

The site was part of the broader Cahuenga Valley ranch and orchard lands — semi-rural, with farms, grazing, and scattered horticultural operations dotting the dry hills before the real-estate subdividers arrived.

The 1877 Map and the James Lick Tract

An 1877 land ownership map of the Cahuenga Valley pushes the documented history of the future 1746 N Gramercy parcel back eleven years before the 1888 map. The map shows the land labeled: James Lick — 1,500 Acres, directly where the future Gramercy Place neighborhood would be built.

James Lick (1796–1876) was at the time of his death the wealthiest man in California — a Pennsylvania-born carpenter's son who built a piano-making fortune in South America, arrived in San Francisco just before the Gold Rush, and parlayed the resulting real estate boom into an estimated $4–5 million fortune. He owned large areas of Santa Clara County, all of Santa Catalina Island, and approximately 1,500 acres in the Cahuenga Valley. Lick died October 1, 1876 — leaving his estate to fund a series of public bequests, most celebrated of which was the Lick Observatory on Mount Hamilton — the first major astronomical observatory on the West Coast.

The land under 1746 N Gramercy Place was once, in short, part of the endowment that built the observatory where humanity first systematically studied the stars.

The 1888 Map: Busseyer Land & the Railroad

By 1888, the same parcel appears under the Busseyer family's name — the first identified private owners of what would become the 1746 N Gramercy lot, likely German immigrant farmers typical of the Cahuenga Valley in the 1880s. Surrounding landowners include Mrs. Groket, Neiet, Sullivan, Barrow, and Lopez — a patchwork of small private holdings.

Also visible on the 1888 map: a diagonal rail line running along what becomes Hollywood Boulevard. This is the Cahuenga Valley Railroad, which built its tracks along Hollywood Boulevard between Western and Wilcox in 1888, connecting the remote Cahuenga Valley to downtown Los Angeles for the first time. Without this railroad, Jeremiah Morgan's 1905 subdivision — and the house at 1746 N Gramercy — might never have been built when they were.

The Water Lily Nursery & Immaculate Heart

Nearby on Franklin Avenue, Edmund D. Sturtevant operated his commercial water lily nursery — relocated to Hollywood ca. 1894 — featuring large fenced ponds of sacred Egyptian lotus and water lilies set amid the dry hills, contributing to Hollywood's early reputation as a garden-filled picturesque suburb.

Just blocks north, the Sisters of the Immaculate Heart of Mary broke ground on April 24, 1905 on what would become Immaculate Heart High School. At the time the site sat outside the city limits entirely, flanked by olive and orange trees — confirming the semi-rural character of the neighborhood at the very moment Jeremiah Morgan was platting the lots along Gramercy Place.

Chapter II

Jeremiah J. Morgan & the Creation of Morgan Place

1903 – 1926

The street that became Gramercy Place was created by one man: Jeremiah J. "Jerry" Morgan — Civil War veteran, Iowa cattle rancher, and Los Angeles real-estate developer. Born in Illinois in 1841, Morgan first visited Los Angeles during the land boom of 1886 and settled permanently in February 1902.

Morgan was already 64 years old when he began platting the tract. His personal life was as colorful as his business career: after divorcing his longtime wife, he married Joeanna (Annie) Wagner — a 20-year-old telephone operator — on July 10, 1909. Canny to the last, he had his young bride sign a prenuptial agreement barring any claim on his estate in exchange for $5,000 cash. He died May 31, 1925, age 84, leaving an estate valued at approximately $1,250,000 — with bequests conditioned on relatives becoming Christians.

The Subdivision

In 1905, Morgan formally subdivided Morgan's Hollywood Tract — approximately 10 acres of former ranch and orchard land. The tract created 97 lots along a new street he named Morgan Place, running north-south through the center. The lots were 50 feet wide by 200 feet deep on a street 80 feet wide, improved with cement sidewalks and shade trees. The lot that became 1746 N Gramercy was Lot 58 and the south 25 feet of Lot 59.

"The magnificent gateway, erected at a large cost, facing the car line, being the handsomest of its kind in Hollywood."

Los Angeles Times advertisement, Morgan's Hollywood Tract, c. 1905–1911

Morgan Place Becomes Gramercy Place

Morgan died in May 1925. In May 1926, the Los Angeles City Planning Commission proposed changing Morgan Place to Gramercy Place. A group calling itself the "Hollywood Boulevard, Western to Cahuenga, Improvement Association" formally protested — getting his middle initial wrong, calling him "Jeremiah P. Morgan" (it was J). The protest was unsuccessful. Ironically, the renaming happened just one year before Dorothy Lohman's Pi Beta Phi sorority record first places the Lohman family at the address in March 1927.

Chapter III

What the Sanborn Maps Show

March 1907 & January 1913

Hollywood in 1907 was not yet the suburb it would become, let alone the film capital. The Sanborn Fire Insurance Atlas documents its commercial character with precision: a Hollywood Fuel & Feed Yard, the Pacific Mercantile Co., D.I. Noftziger Lumber (valued at $300,000), a Skating Rink, and the Church of the Blessed Sacrament. There is not a single film-related building anywhere on the 1907 Hollywood Sanborn maps — the entire industry that would define the neighborhood was still four years away.

March 1907 Sanborn — Sheet 14
  • Future 1746 N Gramercy lot: completely vacant
  • Surrounding blocks labeled "TO BE SUBDIVIDED SOON"

Chapter IV

The Film Industry at the Doorstep

1911 – 1981

By the time the house at 1746 N Gramercy Place went up, the world immediately around it was transforming at a pace its first residents could barely have anticipated.

Oct 27, 1911

The Nestor Film Company — the Horsley brothers — rented an abandoned tavern at the northwest corner of Sunset and Gower for $30 a month. They converted the old Blondeau Tavern's bar into a carpenter shop, its dining rooms into dressing rooms. Local residents were scandalized — signs appeared at nearby hotels reading "No Dogs, No Actors." This was 8 blocks from 1746 N Gramercy Place.

April 1911

Moving Picture World published a landmark article by Richard V. Spencer titled "Los Angeles as a Producing Center," citing 320 days per year suitable for photography. He named studios operating within miles of the future Gramercy Place neighborhood: Selig at Edendale with a $75,000 plant, Pathé West Coast, Bison Studio, Biograph, Kalem near Glendale.

"It is predicted that Los Angeles will become known to the world not as the second largest picture producing center, but the largest, bar none."

Richard V. Spencer · Moving Picture World · April 1911 · Six months before Nestor opened at Sunset & Gower
Dec 1913

Cecil B. DeMille, Samuel Goldwyn, and Jesse Lasky rented a horse barn at the corner of Selma and Vine Streets — 10 blocks from 1746 N Gramercy — and produced The Squaw Man, the first feature-length film made in Hollywood. That barn became the seed of what is now Paramount Pictures.

1981 / 1983

The American Film Institute moved its Conservatory to the former Immaculate Heart College campus at 2021 North Western Avenue — the same campus whose high school had broken ground in April 1905, in the same season Jeremiah Morgan was platting the lots along Gramercy Place. The land that was a feed yard and mercantile store in 1907 is now home to the film industry's most celebrated graduate school.

The Neighborhood & Hollywood's Birth

The neighborhood's homes and Hollywood's first studios were being built simultaneously, by the same builders, from the same lumber yards. All of it — houses and studio alike — was built from the same yellow wood-frame timber construction visible throughout both Sanborn surveys.

Hollywood's first residents went from skating rinks to movie theaters in the span of two years.

Chapter V

The Lohman Family Era

By March 1927 – 1950s

By the late 1920s, the house was home to the Lohman family, a middle-to-upper-middle-class household representative of early Hollywood's professional residents. The family's presence at 1746 N Gramercy can be precisely dated to at least March 22, 1927 — the date daughter Dorothy was initiated into the Pi Beta Phi sorority at USC, with her address listed as "1746 N. Gramercy, Hollywood, Calif."

Luther C. Lohman
1866 – 1937  ·  Head of Household
Born Minnesota to German immigrants. Co-owner of Lohman Brothers Plumbing at 105 E 2nd Street with brother Theodore. By 1920 working as plumbing salesman "on his own account." Owned the home; died 1937.
Anna Margaret Young Lohman
1875 – 1954  ·  Wife, Businesswoman
Far from a passive housewife — the 1916 city directory confirms she was managing multiple apartment properties simultaneously while Luther ran the plumbing business. Became head of household after Luther's 1937 death.
Dorothy Margaret Lohman Harrison
March 23, 1907 – March 14, 1960
Initiated Pi Beta Phi at USC, March 22, 1927 — earliest confirmed Lohman occupancy date. Hosted bridal shower at house November 1932. Married attorney George Harrison 1933. Still at 1746 N Gramercy with family through February 1942.
George Revelle Harrison
Jan 31, 1904 – 1960  ·  Attorney
Dorothy's husband. Offices at 639 S. Spring St., Los Angeles. Physically: 6'1", 195 lbs, blue eyes, blonde hair. WWII Draft Card (Feb 15, 1942) lists 1746 N Gramercy — last confirmed record at address.
John Tyrell Lohman
1900 – 1967  ·  Son
Despite marrying in 1925, still living at 1746 N Gramercy in 1940 at age 40 — listed as a plumber. Returned to or never fully left the family home.
Elsie Holle
Born c.1916 · Kansas  ·  Live-in Maid
Confirmed in the 1940 census. Replaced Louise Bealin (live-in maid, 1930 census) as the household's domestic staff.

The 1940 Household — Three Generations Under One Roof

The 1940 U.S. Census paints the fullest picture of life at 1746 N Gramercy: seven people across three generations — Anna Lohman (widowed head, 62), son John (40, plumber), daughter Dorothy (33), son-in-law George (36, attorney), daughter Revelle (5 — incorrectly recorded as male by the enumerator, confirmed female in 1950), infant daughter Ann (½), and maid Elsie Holle (24).

All household members listed their 1935 residence as "Same House" — meaning the entire multi-generational family had been at 1746 N Gramercy since at least 1935, two years before Luther's death.

The Marsden Apartments — Across the Street

Directly across Gramercy Place at 1745 N Gramercy stood the Marsden Apartments — an Art Deco building completed in 1935, designed by architect Frank Green, built by Charlotte Pickford's estate (Mary Pickford's mother). 68 single apartments, 22 double apartments, and a 3-bedroom penthouse.

Los Angeles historic preservation reports state that Jack Benny (1894–1974) is said to have used the Marsden penthouse as his Hollywood pied-à-terre during his NBC radio years. NBC Radio City opened October 17, 1938 at Sunset and Vine — just 6 blocks away. The overlap window with the Lohman-Harrison family directly across the street at 1746 is specific: from the Marsden's 1935 completion through at least February 1942, Benny's reported penthouse use and the Harrison household's documented occupancy run in direct parallel, separated only by the width of Gramercy Place.

Chapter VI

Jules G. Salkin — Developer, Musician, Architect, Fugitive

1956 – 1980

The man who built the current apartment building at 1746 N Gramercy was one of the most extraordinary — and ultimately tragic — figures in postwar Los Angeles history. Jules G. Salkin (1916–1998) was simultaneously a concert violist, WWII paratrooper, Hollywood studio musician, licensed architect, attorney, real-estate developer, and co-founder of one of LA's most celebrated modernist housing cooperatives.

  • The Musician: First-chair violist with the Indianapolis Symphony. Played viola in the Taliesin String Quartet during summers of 1938 and 1939 at Frank Lloyd Wright's Taliesin Fellowship in Wisconsin — where he met architect John Lautner.
  • The Architect: Licensed architect 1954; AIA member 1957. Co-founded the Mutual Housing Association (MHA) responsible for the landmark Crestwood Hills cooperative development in Brentwood, involving architects A. Quincy Jones and Whitney Smith.
  • The Lautner Connection: Commissioned John Lautner to design his own home at 1430 Avon Terrace in Echo Park (built 1948), now designated Los Angeles Historic-Cultural Monument No. 1111 — known as the "Lost Lautner."
  • The Scandal: Misappropriated funds from MHA during Crestwood Hills. In 1968 indicted for defrauding the SBA of $445,000, compounded by tax evasion. Rather than face prosecution, Salkin fled to England.
  • The Fugitive: Lived in England 12 years, working in construction and playing viola with orchestras. Returned under a false passport. FBI arrested him on return. Pleaded guilty — Los Angeles Times, April 2, 1980. Sentenced to 18 months in prison — Los Angeles Times, June 3, 1980.
The Erasure

The LA City Planning Department's own assessment is blunt: "Jules Salkin was a prominent real estate developer but is largely forgotten due to a financial scandal that forced him to move abroad."

The 22-unit apartment building at 1746 N Gramercy Place — built in 1956 — now stands as one of the few surviving physical traces of a man who helped shape postwar Los Angeles and then vanished from its history.

Chapter VII

Joseph Chase Murphy & the Hidden Queer History

Late 1950s – Post-2000

The apartment building Jules Salkin constructed in 1956 quickly acquired a social identity no official record would ever capture. Its story from the late 1950s onward is inseparable from the hidden queer history of Hollywood.

One Block West

At the corner of Hollywood Boulevard and Van Ness Avenue stood the Richfield Oil gas station at 5777 Hollywood Boulevard — made legendary by Scotty Bowers, a WWII Marine veteran who from 1946 to 1950 used it as the hub of Hollywood's covert gay underground. The station served closeted stars including Cary Grant, Spencer Tracy, Katharine Hepburn, Charles Laughton, Tennessee Williams and Gore Vidal — during an era when the LAPD vice squad ran what one documentary director described as a "fascistic sexual gestapo." The plot is now a fire station.

Club Tarimino

Under the ownership of Joseph Chase Murphy — a gay man who came to Los Angeles in the 1940s and worked in architecture during the postwar building boom — 1746 N Gramercy Place became known among residents and community as "Club Tarimino." In a neighborhood already quietly networked into Hollywood's queer underground through the Richfield station one block away, Murphy's stewardship created a safe harbor for gay residents during an era when such safety was genuinely hard to find.

1980s

The AIDS epidemic arrived at 1746 N Gramercy as it did across Hollywood. A previous resident, Mavis Anderson, shared first-hand testimony that she knew AIDS victims among former residents who had survived into the mid-1990s — placing the building directly within the human story of that epidemic's devastating passage through the gay community of Hollywood.

Mid-1990s

Murphy began renting units to students from the American Film Institute Conservatory — located at the former Immaculate Heart College campus on Western Avenue, just blocks away — informally dubbing the building the "AFI dorm." This brought 1746 N Gramercy into direct connection with the institution that occupies the hill above Gramercy Place, closing yet another circle in the building's long relationship with the film world born at Sunset and Gower in 1911.

Post-2000

Murphy died sometime after 2000, leaving the property to Tony Diaz, his longtime maintenance man, whose family now lives in and maintains the apartments. The continuity of stewardship from Murphy to Diaz represents the building's most recent chapter — a property that passed not through commercial transaction but through personal loyalty and trust.

A Note on Sources

The oral history in this section derives from the first-hand testimony of a resident and visitor to 1746 N Gramercy Place in 1998, and from conversations with Mavis Anderson, a previous resident. It is presented as oral history rather than documented record — and is the more valuable for it. Primary witness accounts of hidden social history are irreplaceable, precisely because official records never captured this world at all.

Appendix

Research Gaps Worth Pursuing

  1. The exact identity and history of the Busseyer family — LA County deed records from the 1880s–1900s would trace when and how the land passed from Busseyer to Morgan.
  2. Who occupied the house between its construction (~1910–1912) and the Lohmans' confirmed arrival in March 1927? City directories from 1914–1926 would fill this 15-year gap.
  3. The exact year the Harrison family vacated 1746 N Gramercy (between February 1942 and April 1950) and who if anyone occupied it in those final years before demolition.
  4. Jack Benny's precise dates of residence at the Marsden penthouse (1745 N Gramercy) and whether his tenure overlapped with the Lohman-Harrison family at 1746.
  5. The exact nature of the "J. Di Maggio" business listing on Hollywood Blvd at Gramercy Place visible on the late-1950s cross-directory map.
  6. The exact timeline of Jules Salkin's financial scandal — specifically when the MHA misappropriation occurred relative to his 1956 construction of 1746 N Gramercy.
  7. Whether the 1960 Tokyo orphans contact listing corresponds to a specific tenant — a 1960 city directory cross-reference would confirm this.
  8. Further documentation on Revelle W. Harrison's life after 1950, and the subsequent histories of John R. and Nina S. Harrison.
