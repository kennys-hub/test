Part I

Scattered Actualities & Scenics

1895 – 1906

1895 – 1897

Motion pictures debut commercially in the United States. Production is concentrated in major urban centers, especially New York City and Chicago. Early filmmaking depends on proximity to theatrical talent, equipment manufacturers, distribution exchanges, and urban audiences. Southern California is absent from trade-press discussions of production centers.

1898 – 1902

Cameramen from companies such as the Edison Manufacturing Company and the Biograph Company travel west occasionally to capture short "actualities" and scenic footage. Subjects include Pasadena parades, orange groves, and harbor scenes in Los Angeles. These films are non-narrative and produced by itinerant cameramen.

No production infrastructure exists in Southern California. The industry's geographic center remains the New York region, particularly Fort Lee, New Jersey.

1903 – 1906

Occasional scenic footage continues in California, but narrative filmmaking remains Eastern-based. Fort Lee becomes the dominant early production hub because of its proximity to New York studios, inexpensive land, and dramatic Palisades landscapes. Southern California remains geographically peripheral to the industry.

Part II

The Turn West

1907 – 1909  ·  Narrative Production Begins

1907

Francis Boggs of the Selig Polyscope Company begins narrative production in Los Angeles. Early filming reportedly occurs on a rooftop studio near Main Street and 8th Street in downtown Los Angeles. His goal is pragmatic rather than visionary: to exploit year-round outdoor filming conditions.

This represents the first sustained narrative production unit operating in the Los Angeles region — the first real test of California as a film-production base.

Crucially, Selig's own commercial catalog from this same year reveals that the company had already been sending camera operators to California before Boggs arrived. The Selig Polyscope 1907 Film Catalog — a primary source document now held at the Internet Archive — lists multiple Los Angeles–shot subjects in its standing film library, including No. 441 "Pigeon Farm at Los Angeles" and No. 326 "Parade of Roses", described as filmed at "the last Fiesta held in Los Angeles, California." These were actuality and scenic subjects shot by Selig cameramen dispatched from Chicago — evidence that the company was treating California as a filming destination in the years immediately preceding Boggs's narrative work.

The catalog also documents Selig's extensive location-filming reach across the American West in 1906–07: Colorado mountain railroads, Wyoming reservation dances, and dramatic scenics throughout the Rockies — shot under formal permits and railroad cooperation. This organizational infrastructure for distant location work is what made the California assignment logistically feasible.

"One of the most beautiful features at the last Fiesta held in Los Angeles, California. Carriages of every kind and description pass by, literally covered with roses and other flowers. No scene ever taken contains more beauty in such a comparatively limited space. This picture never fails to prove one of the hits of the evening."

Selig Polyscope Company Film Catalog, 1907  ·  No. 326 "Parade of Roses" (50 ft.)  —  archive.org/details/MotionPictureCatalogI156

The catalog further demonstrates that No. 115 "Holdup of the Leadville Stage" was explicitly filmed on location in Colorado — "negatives were made in Colorado on the old Leadville stage road through the Garden of the Gods and Ute Pass" — confirming Selig's established practice of sending crews far afield for narrative subjects, not just actualities. Boggs's Los Angeles assignment in 1907 was an extension of this already-functioning model, not an unprecedented leap.

Late 1907 · Monte Cristo

In late 1907, director Francis Boggs and cameraman Thomas Persons travel from Chicago to Los Angeles to complete exterior scenes for Monte Cristo (also known as The Count of Monte Cristo), a one-reel Selig adaptation starring Hobart Bosworth. Interiors are shot at Selig's Chicago studio.

The pivotal scene — Edmond Dantès emerging from the sea after his prison escape — requires an ocean location. Boggs films it on the Southern California coast. The ocean location is genuinely disputed in the primary sources: Selig biographer Andrew Erish argues for Laguna Beach's Three Arches rock formation; the AFI Catalog lists "Laguna Beach, Santa Monica, or Venice" as possibilities; and Mrs. Hobart Bosworth, writing in Variety in January 1958, stated flatly that "The 1907 filming of Monte Cristo was carried on at the beach town of Venice." No single source has definitively closed the question. The AFI Catalog records the film's release date as January 30, 1908.

For the interior-style scenes, Boggs and Persons rented the roof of a two-story structure on Main Street between 7th and 8th. The AFI Catalog entry for Monte Cristo confirms this address directly, recording that the production "constructed a temporary Selig set on the roof of a building on Main Street between 7th and 8th streets." Per Otis Turner writing in the Highland Park Herald in 1916, the building owner threw in several small unused rooms below as dressing rooms. A 1926 Los Angeles Times account described it as "the tinsmith's roof studio, an ideal location with nothing to interfere with sunlight." This was not a studio. It was a temporary rooftop rig, built for one film, on one trip, then abandoned when Boggs returned to Chicago. It is entirely distinct from — and should never be confused with — the Sing Kee laundry lot Selig established two years later on Olive Street.

Monte Cristo is widely cited as the first narrative film shot in the greater Los Angeles area. Boggs returned to Chicago after completing it — this was a single production trip, not yet a permanent operation.

Simultaneous Development Elsewhere

Jacksonville emerges as a winter filmmaking hub. Companies such as the Kalem Company begin producing films there. Trade-journal commentary in The Moving Picture World offered sustained coverage of Jacksonville's growing operations across multiple volumes:

"The Kalem Company's Florida unit continues to produce excellent subjects under ideal conditions in Jacksonville, with mild weather allowing uninterrupted exteriors impossible in the North. Beaches, rivers, and historic sites provide authentic settings for marine dramas and Southern stories, while the stock company delivers high-quality one-reelers at a rapid pace."

The Moving Picture World, Vol. 9  ·  July–September 1911  —  archive.org/details/moviwor09chal

"The Kalem Company has been receiving many flattering testimonials from exhibitors regarding the quality of their Florida-made subjects. One enthusiastic convert from Jacksonville, Fla., writes: 'Your Florida pictures are the best I have seen — authentic Southern backgrounds and perfect weather make them stand out.'"

The Moving Picture World, Vol. 10  ·  October–December 1911  —  archive.org/details/moviwor10chal  ·  Search: "Kalem" + "Jacksonville"

These statements show that Florida was actively promoted as a rival production center. Notably, both volumes appeared during the same months that Richard V. Spencer was publishing his predictions about Los Angeles — suggesting the trade press was simultaneously boosting both cities as viable production destinations.

Part III

Permanent Footing

1909 – 1911  ·  Infrastructure Emerges

1909 · Sing Kee Laundry Lot

In 1909, Colonel Selig sends Boggs back to Los Angeles — this time with a full company of actors and cameraman James Crosby. After stopping first in New Orleans, they establish what the caption of a contemporary photograph explicitly describes as "permanent facilities" in the rear lot of the Sing Kee Chinese Laundry on Olive Street between 7th and 8th Streets. The exact arrival date is documented: Moving Picture World (January 1922) records that industry figure James McGee "was with the Selig company that established a studio at Eighth and Olive Streets on March 23, 1909."

The anchor primary source for this location is Hobart Bosworth himself. In a 1936 letter, Bosworth wrote: "The 'studio' for these two was a Chinese laundry on Olive near Eighth." His wife, writing in Variety in 1957, confirmed the same, describing it as "a Chinese laundry at Eight and Olive Streets." A 1940 newspaper column by Bill Henry recorded that Boggs "filmed The Power of the Sultan in the drying yard of a Chinese laundry at Eighth and Olive in May 1909." Selig biographer Andrew Erish places The Heart of a Race Tout as filmed in April 1909 "behind the Chinese laundry, in nearby Central Park (later Pershing Square), and at Santa Anita Park racetrack." E.J. Stephens and Marc Wanamaker — drawing directly on the Bison Archives photograph collection — reproduce a contemporary photograph of the lot in active production, captioning it as "permanent facilities" in the rear of the Sing Kee Chinese Laundry on Olive Street between Seventh and Eighth Streets (Early Poverty Row Studios, Arcadia Publishing, 2014, p. 9).

This location is distinct from the 1907 rooftop in every sense — different street, different year, and a fundamentally different type of space. The 1907 setup was an elevated rooftop rig on Main Street between 7th and 8th, chosen for unobstructed sunlight. The Sing Kee lot is a ground-level open-air drying yard — the rear lot of a working laundry, where clothes were hung to dry — on Olive Street between 7th and 8th, just one block west. The two sites are close enough that Boggs clearly knew the neighborhood from his 1907 visit, but they are physically and functionally entirely different spaces. When he returned in 1909 he came back to the same downtown block — this time with a full company, a cameraman, and the intention of sustained production rather than a single-film shoot. Stephens and Wanamaker, drawing on the Bison Archives photograph collection, confirm the 1909 setup as "permanent facilities." Films confirmed shot here include The Heart of a Race Tout, The Peasant Prince, and In the Sultan's Power — the last of which is sometimes cited as the first film shot entirely in Los Angeles.

This marks Selig's transition from single-trip location work to fixed production infrastructure in the city.

1910

Production activity expands rapidly across Los Angeles. Important early facilities include:

  • Edendale — Selig opens a larger studio there.
  • A makeshift studio used by D. W. Griffith of the Biograph Company operates inside a Pacific Electric trolley yard in downtown Los Angeles.
  • Bison Film Company establishes facilities at 1712 Allesandro Street.

The Edendale Production Colony (1909–1928)

In 1909, Selig and Boggs relocated their operation to Edendale — a neighborhood about three miles northwest of downtown Los Angeles, centered on what is now the Echo Park and Silver Lake corridor. The company arrived on August 24, 1909 (per Erish), with Boggs setting up first at his rented bungalow at 1845 Allesandro Street (now Glendale Boulevard). Construction of the permanent studio building was already underway: The Film Index reported in February 1910 that Selig was building "a studio for negatives, 60x120 feet, for $15,000 to $20,000." The completed mission-style building's façade was modeled after the San Gabriel Mission and built with genuine adobe. It was the first permanent motion picture studio in Los Angeles. Inside: stages, dressing rooms, offices, a film laboratory, and an enormous sunken water tank — at the time described as the second-largest glass studio of its kind in the world. Erish identifies Brought to Terms (1909) as "the first film produced at the first permanent studio in Los Angeles."

Selig was quickly followed. New York Motion Picture Company (Bison Pictures) arrived at 1719 Allesandro in 1909. Pathé Frères followed. Keystone Studios — founded by Mack Sennett in 1912 at 1712 Allesandro — became the colony's most famous tenant. Charlie Chaplin filmed his entire first year in the industry at Keystone, including his debut screen appearance, beside those mission-style walls. Roscoe Arbuckle made his film debut at Selig. The first pie-in-the-face scene in cinema — Mabel Normand hitting Arbuckle in A Noise from the Deep (1913) — was filmed at Keystone. By the mid-1910s, a five-block stretch of Allesandro supported roughly thirty studios, lots, and related operations.

The trade press explained the appeal directly. Motography described Edendale in July 1911:

"It is the motion picture center of the Pacific Coast. With clear air and sunshine three hundred days out of the year, conditions are ideal for perfect picture making. The scenic advantages of the location, too, are unique. From Edendale can be seen the Pacific Ocean, twenty-two miles to the west, and the broad panorama of Southern California, with its fruit and stock ranches, its snowcapped mountains and its tropical vegetation. Within a short distance of Edendale may be found every known variety of national scenery, seemingly arranged by a master producer expressly for the motion picture camera."

Motography  ·  July 1911  ·  via Historic Echo Park

Director Jack White captured the operational logic in a single sentence: the first companies settled in Edendale because it offered "Indians, Cowboys, open country." The hills, canyons, and winding roads were not incidental to early filmmaking — they were its visual language. The Keystone chase comedy was inseparable from the landscape that produced it.

Geography as Destiny — Why Edendale Lost to Hollywood

The geography that made Edendale cinematically productive also set a hard ceiling on its growth. Around 1912–13, the format of filmmaking changed. The industry shifted from one-reelers (10–15 minutes) to feature-length films (60+ minutes) — and the physical requirements of production transformed entirely. Edendale's hills became walls.

  • Stages: Features required large, controlled interior stages for sustained lighting. The narrow canyon lots of Edendale could not accommodate them.
  • Backlots: Multi-week productions needed permanent outdoor sets — built towns left standing for months. Hillside terrain made this structurally impossible.
  • Logistics: Features demanded warehouses for costumes, props, and heavy equipment. The winding Edendale roads were a delivery burden that flat Hollywood streets were not.
  • Flat land and price: Hollywood in the early 1910s was still apricot and lemon groves — cheap, flat agricultural land. Where Edendale offered fractions of an acre on hillside lots, a studio in Hollywood could acquire 10 or 20 open acres and build at scale.

The numbers make this tangible. When William Fox first operated out of the old Selig Edendale lot, he had 0.75 acres. Within a year, he opened Fox Studios in Hollywood on a 15-acre lot — twenty times the footprint. Selig himself, when he finally needed more space in 1913, did not move to Hollywood either: he acquired 32 acres in Lincoln Heights to house the zoo animals needed for his jungle serials — an operation that simply could not fit on the canyon-side lots of Edendale.

The exodus began earlier than is often recognized. Studios started leaving Edendale for Hollywood as early as 1911 — just a year and a half after Hollywood was absorbed into the City of Los Angeles. Mack Sennett finally moved his entire operation from Edendale to Studio City in 1928, closing the last chapter of the colony.

"Edendale gave the movies their look. Hollywood gave them their scale."

Geography as Destiny — the Edendale thesis

The transition is crystallized on a single date: October 27, 1911. That morning, Francis Boggs — the man who had built the Edendale studio and directed over 200 films there — was shot dead on the studio lot by a deranged former employee. That same afternoon, David Horsley and Al Christie opened Nestor Studios at Sunset and Gower in Hollywood. Edendale's founding pioneer killed; Hollywood's first studio opened — on the same calendar day.

1911 — Trade-Press Recognition

By 1911, industry observers begin publicly recognizing Los Angeles's potential. In April 1911, The Moving Picture World published an article titled "Los Angeles as a Producing Center" by trade writer Richard V. Spencer. Spencer described Southern California's advantages: mountains shielding the region from storms, nearby beaches, varied landscapes, and abundant sunlight. He estimated roughly 320 workable filming days per year.

"By reason of the advantages set forth, it is predicted that Los Angeles will become known to the world not as the second largest picture producing center, but the largest, bar none."

Richard V. Spencer  ·  The Moving Picture World, April 1911

At the time, this was speculative rather than consensus.

October 27, 1911

The Nestor Film Company opens the first permanent studio in Hollywood at Sunset Boulevard and Gower Street. Hollywood was chosen because of inexpensive land, agricultural zoning, rail access, and fewer municipal restrictions than downtown Los Angeles.

December 1913

Cecil B. DeMille and Jesse L. Lasky establish a studio inside a rented barn at Selma Avenue and Vine Street. This site later becomes known as the Lasky-DeMille Barn.

Part IV

The Patent Trust Factor

1908 – 1915

1908 – 1912

The formation of the Motion Picture Patents Company (MPPC) — commonly called the Edison Trust — reshapes the entire geography of American filmmaking. Thomas Edison and his partners hold a near-total monopoly on motion picture cameras, projectors, and raw film stock. Any filmmaker who refused to pay Trust royalties faced not only litigation but physical intimidation.

Edison employed what the industry called "goon squads" — enforcers who traveled to independent production locations, confiscated cameras, destroyed equipment, and in some cases assaulted crew members. The threat was real and constant. Independent producers operating in New York and New Jersey were within easy reach of Trust investigators, who monitored studios, tracked equipment purchases, and used the courts and hired muscle in tandem.

The response was flight. Independent producers began moving their operations as far from New York as geography allowed — first to Florida, then to Cuba, and eventually to the American West. Distance itself became a competitive advantage: the further from New York, the longer it took goon squads to arrive, and the more time a crew had to finish shooting before being shut down.

Southern California — nearly 3,000 miles from Edison's New Jersey base — offered:

  • Maximum geographic distance from Trust enforcement agents
  • A quick escape route to Mexico if investigators appeared on set
  • Large open spaces with no neighbors to report suspicious activity
  • Reliable weather for year-round production — maximizing output between raids

The western migration was not, at first, about sunlight or scenery. It was about survival.

Part IV-B

The Moguls — Who Actually Built Hollywood

1908 – 1920s

The Men

The independent producers who fled the Edison Trust and built Hollywood were not, for the most part, established American businessmen. They were Eastern European Jewish immigrants — men who had arrived in America with little money and found themselves locked out of the country's established industries by the social and institutional anti-Semitism of the era. Banking, law, academia, and the established press were largely closed to them. The film business, considered low-status and disreputable, was one of the few entrepreneurial spaces where outsiders could compete.

The Edison Trust itself was dominated by old-money Protestant establishment figures. The independents fleeing west were escaping two monopolies simultaneously — Edison's legal and physical stranglehold on film technology, and the broader social exclusion that kept Jewish entrepreneurs out of more respectable industries. Hollywood became, in the words of historian Neal Gabler, an industry built by outsiders, for outsiders — a place where men who could not join existing American institutions simply built their own.

Among those who made the journey west: Jesse Lasky, Samuel Goldfish (later Goldwyn), Adolph Zukor, Louis B. Mayer, and William Fox. They would go on to found or shape every major studio that defined the golden age of American cinema.

1913 · Flagstaff

The story is often told as an accident — but the full picture is more precise. By late 1913, Los Angeles was already an established film city: the Edendale colony had been operating for three years, Selig had studios in downtown LA, Griffith had filmed there, and Richard V. Spencer had already declared in print that LA would become the world's largest film center. When Jesse Lasky and Samuel Goldfish sent director Cecil B. DeMille west to shoot a feature-length Western called The Squaw Man, they first tried Flagstaff, Arizona — rugged terrain, far from Edison's reach, accessible by rail.

DeMille arrived, decided Flagstaff was wrong for the picture, got back on the train, and rode it to Los Angeles. He knew the city was a film destination. What he did not know — what nobody yet knew — was that the cheap barn he found on Selma Avenue and Vine Street sat in a quiet agricultural suburb called Hollywood, not in the established Edendale film zone a few miles east. Hollywood had exactly one studio at the time: Nestor, open barely two years at Sunset and Gower. It was not yet the epicenter of anything.

DeMille's telegram back to New York captures this perfectly. He does not say "have arrived in Los Angeles." He says:

"Flagstaff no good for our purposes. Have rented a barn in a place called Hollywood."

Cecil B. DeMille · Telegram to Jesse Lasky & Samuel Goldfish · Late 1913
Dec 1913 · The Barn

The barn DeMille rented stood at the corner of Selma Avenue and Vine Street — a working horse barn converted into a film studio for a few dollars a month. There, DeMille shot The Squaw Man, which became the first feature-length film ever made in Hollywood. It was not a polished operation. The barn was drafty, the equipment rudimentary, and the neighborhood still largely agricultural. But the film was completed, distributed, and successful — and the barn became the seed of what is now Paramount Pictures, whose lot at 5555 Melrose Avenue remains the only major studio still physically located in Hollywood today.

The site is now preserved as the Hollywood Heritage Museum — the Lasky-DeMille Barn — at 2100 N. Highland Avenue, where it was relocated in 1983.

1915 · Freedom

In 1915, the United States Supreme Court ruled that the Motion Picture Patents Company constituted an illegal monopoly in restraint of trade and ordered its dissolution. The Edison Trust — the force that had driven independent filmmakers across a continent — was broken.

The men who had fled east to west were now free to build openly, legally, and at scale. Within the next decade they founded or consolidated the institutions that defined American cinema for a century:

  • Universal Pictures — Carl Laemmle, who had fought the Trust openly for years
  • Paramount Pictures — grown from the Lasky-DeMille barn at Selma & Vine
  • 20th Century Fox — William Fox, who had been a Trust target since 1908
  • MGM — built in part by Louis B. Mayer, who had operated underground in New England

The dream factory that emerged from a $30-a-month tavern at Sunset and Gower, a rented barn at Selma and Vine, and a telegram from Flagstaff was now the most powerful entertainment industry on earth — built almost entirely by men who had been told, in one form or another, that they did not belong.

Part V

The Competition Years

1908 – 1915

1908 – 1915

During this period the American film industry remains geographically fluid. Competing production centers include Jacksonville, San Antonio, New Orleans, and Phoenix. Jacksonville becomes the most significant rival.

By the mid-1910s roughly 25–30 studios operate there seasonally, and the city is sometimes described as the "Winter Film Capital."

However, Florida production faces structural limits:

  • Seasonal operations
  • Limited terrain diversity
  • Civic resistance to film crews
Part VI

Industrial Lock-In

1912 – 1915

1912 – 1915

Between 1912 and 1915 the industry consolidates in Southern California. Reinforcing forces include accelerating studio construction, clustering of labor and suppliers, real-estate development supporting studios, and increasing demand for consistent outdoor locations for feature films.

Once this agglomeration economy forms, relocation becomes increasingly difficult. By 1915, Los Angeles becomes the dominant American film-production center.

Key Insights
  • Edendale's hills created early cinema's visual language — chase comedies, canyon scenics, winding roads — but those same hills became physical walls against expansion once the feature-film format demanded flat acreage, large stages, and backlots. Edendale gave the movies their look; Hollywood gave them their scale.
  • The transition is crystallized on a single date: October 27, 1911 — Francis Boggs killed at Edendale; Nestor Studios opens in Hollywood the same afternoon.
  • Before 1905, Southern California was not seriously considered as a production base.
  • The Selig 1907 catalog reveals the company had dispatched cameramen to Los Angeles for actuality subjects before Boggs's 1907 narrative assignment — making Selig's California presence a deliberate organizational practice, not a single individual's initiative.
  • Between 1907 and 1912, multiple cities competed to host the industry — including Jacksonville, Cuba, San Antonio, New Orleans, and Phoenix.
  • The westward migration was driven first by physical survival — escaping Edison's goon squads — and only secondarily by climate and scenery.
  • The founding moguls were overwhelmingly Jewish immigrant entrepreneurs escaping two monopolies at once: Edison's patent stranglehold and East Coast social exclusion. They built Hollywood because established America had no place for them.
  • DeMille's 1913 Flagstaff telegram is often told as an accident — but LA was already the obvious destination, with Nestor, Edendale, and Griffith all operating there before he arrived. The telegram dramatizes an arrival, not a discovery.
  • Jacksonville and Los Angeles were both promoted in trade journals as ideal filming environments — often in the same volumes of The Moving Picture World, published within weeks of each other.
  • Hollywood's eventual dominance resulted from climate, distance from Edison, industrial clustering, and the 1915 Supreme Court dissolution of the Trust — not from a single strategic decision.
