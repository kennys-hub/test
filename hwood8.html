Part I

Scattered Actualities & Scenics

1895 – 1906

1895 – 1897

Motion pictures debut commercially in the United States. Production is concentrated in major urban centers, especially New York City and Chicago. Early filmmaking depends on proximity to theatrical talent, equipment manufacturers, distribution exchanges, and urban audiences. Southern California is absent from trade-press discussions of production centers.

1898 – 1902

Cameramen from companies such as the Edison Manufacturing Company and the Biograph Company travel west occasionally to capture short "actualities" and scenic footage. Subjects include Pasadena parades, orange groves, and harbor scenes in Los Angeles. These films are non-narrative and produced by itinerant cameramen.

No production infrastructure exists in Southern California. The industry's geographic center remains the New York region, particularly Fort Lee, New Jersey.

1903 – 1906

Occasional scenic footage continues in California, but narrative filmmaking remains Eastern-based. Fort Lee becomes the dominant early production hub because of its proximity to New York studios, inexpensive land, and dramatic Palisades landscapes. Southern California remains geographically peripheral to the industry.

Part II

The Turn West

1907 – 1909  ·  Narrative Production Begins

1907

Francis Boggs of the Selig Polyscope Company begins narrative production in Los Angeles. Early filming reportedly occurs on a rooftop studio near Main Street and 8th Street in downtown Los Angeles. His goal is pragmatic rather than visionary: to exploit year-round outdoor filming conditions.

This represents the first sustained narrative production unit operating in the Los Angeles region.

The Selig Polyscope 1907 Film Catalog — held at the Internet Archive — lists multiple Los Angeles–shot subjects already in the library: No. 441 "Pigeon Farm at Los Angeles" and No. 326 "Parade of Roses", described as filmed at "the last Fiesta held in Los Angeles, California." These were actuality subjects shot by Selig cameramen dispatched from Chicago before Boggs's narrative assignment.

"One of the most beautiful features at the last Fiesta held in Los Angeles, California. Carriages of every kind and description pass by, literally covered with roses and other flowers. No scene ever taken contains more beauty in such a comparatively limited space."

Selig Polyscope Company Film Catalog, 1907  ·  No. 326 "Parade of Roses" (50 ft.)  —  archive.org
Late 1907 · Monte Cristo

Director Francis Boggs and cameraman Thomas Persons travel from Chicago to complete exterior scenes for Monte Cristo, a one-reel Selig adaptation starring Hobart Bosworth. The ocean escape scene is filmed on the Southern California coast — the location is disputed between Laguna Beach (Three Arches) and Venice. The AFI Catalog confirms a temporary set was constructed on the roof of a building on Main Street between 7th and 8th. Released January 30, 1908.

Monte Cristo is widely cited as the first narrative film shot in the greater Los Angeles area. This was a single production trip — Boggs returned to Chicago after completing it.

Simultaneous Development Elsewhere

Jacksonville, Florida emerges as a winter filmmaking hub. The Kalem Company establishes regular production there:

"The Kalem Company's Florida unit continues to produce excellent subjects under ideal conditions in Jacksonville, with mild weather allowing uninterrupted exteriors impossible in the North."

The Moving Picture World, Vol. 9  ·  July–September 1911  —  archive.org

Both volumes of The Moving Picture World promoting Jacksonville appeared during the same months that Richard V. Spencer was publishing predictions about Los Angeles — the trade press was simultaneously boosting both cities.

Part III

Permanent Footing

1909 – 1911  ·  Infrastructure Emerges

1909 · Sing Kee Laundry Lot

Colonel Selig sends Boggs back to Los Angeles with a full company and cameraman James Crosby. They establish "permanent facilities" in the rear lot of the Sing Kee Chinese Laundry on Olive Street between 7th and 8th Streets. Exact date documented: Moving Picture World (January 1922) records the company established at Eighth and Olive on March 23, 1909.

Hobart Bosworth's 1936 letter confirms: "The 'studio' for these two was a Chinese laundry on Olive near Eighth." Films confirmed shot here include The Heart of a Race Tout, The Peasant Prince, and In the Sultan's Power. This marks Selig's transition from single-trip location work to fixed production infrastructure.

1910

Production activity expands rapidly. Important early facilities include:

  • Edendale — Selig opens a larger studio there.
  • A makeshift studio used by D. W. Griffith of the Biograph Company inside a Pacific Electric trolley yard in downtown Los Angeles.
  • Bison Film Company establishes facilities at 1712 Allesandro Street.

The Edendale Production Colony (1909–1928)

In 1909, Selig and Boggs relocated to Edendale — a neighborhood about three miles northwest of downtown LA, centered on what is now the Echo Park and Silver Lake corridor. The company arrived on August 24, 1909, with Boggs setting up first at his rented bungalow at 1845 Allesandro Street (now Glendale Boulevard). Construction of the permanent studio building was already underway. The completed mission-style building was the first permanent motion picture studio in Los Angeles: stages, dressing rooms, offices, a film laboratory, and an enormous sunken water tank.

Keystone Studios — founded by Mack Sennett in 1912 at 1712 Allesandro — became the colony's most famous tenant. Charlie Chaplin filmed his entire first year in the industry at Keystone. The first pie-in-the-face scene in cinema — Mabel Normand hitting Arbuckle in A Noise from the Deep (1913) — was filmed there. By the mid-1910s, a five-block stretch of Allesandro supported roughly thirty studios, lots, and related operations.

"It is the motion picture center of the Pacific Coast. With clear air and sunshine three hundred days out of the year, conditions are ideal for perfect picture making. Within a short distance of Edendale may be found every known variety of national scenery, seemingly arranged by a master producer expressly for the motion picture camera."

Motography  ·  July 1911  ·  via Historic Echo Park

Geography as Destiny — Why Edendale Lost to Hollywood

The geography that made Edendale cinematically productive also set a hard ceiling on its growth. Around 1912–13, the format of filmmaking changed from one-reelers (10–15 minutes) to feature-length films (60+ minutes) — and the physical requirements of production transformed entirely. Edendale's hills became walls.

  • Stages: Features required large, controlled interior stages. The narrow canyon lots of Edendale could not accommodate them.
  • Backlots: Multi-week productions needed permanent outdoor sets left standing for months. Hillside terrain made this structurally impossible.
  • Flat land and price: Hollywood in the early 1910s was apricot and lemon groves — cheap, flat agricultural land. Where Edendale offered fractions of an acre on hillside lots, a Hollywood studio could acquire 10 or 20 open acres.

When William Fox operated out of the old Selig Edendale lot, he had 0.75 acres. Within a year he opened Fox Studios in Hollywood on a 15-acre lot — twenty times the footprint.

"Edendale gave the movies their look. Hollywood gave them their scale."

Geography as Destiny — the Edendale thesis

The transition is crystallized on a single date: October 27, 1911. That morning, Francis Boggs — the man who built the Edendale studio — was shot dead on the studio lot. That same afternoon, David Horsley and Al Christie opened Nestor Studios at Sunset and Gower in Hollywood. Edendale's founding pioneer killed; Hollywood's first studio opened — on the same calendar day.

October 27, 1911

The Nestor Film Company opens the first permanent studio in Hollywood at Sunset Boulevard and Gower Street. Hollywood was chosen because of inexpensive land, agricultural zoning, rail access, and fewer municipal restrictions than downtown Los Angeles.

December 1913

Cecil B. DeMille and Jesse L. Lasky establish a studio inside a rented barn at Selma Avenue and Vine Street — the Lasky-DeMille Barn, seed of Paramount Pictures.

Geographic Audit

The Terrain Verdict

1894 USGS Survey  ·  Why Edendale Could Not Become Hollywood

The shift from Edendale to Hollywood is not typically framed as a geographic argument. It should be. The topographic record — written on a federal government survey completed thirteen years before Boggs arrived and nineteen years before DeMille signed his lease — makes the industrial logic inescapable. Edendale was a canyon. Hollywood was a plain. When the feature film format demanded industrial scale, those two sentences decided everything.

Part IV

The Patent Trust Factor

1908 – 1915

1908 – 1912

The formation of the Motion Picture Patents Company (MPPC) — commonly called the Edison Trust — reshapes the entire geography of American filmmaking. Thomas Edison and his partners hold a near-total monopoly on motion picture cameras, projectors, and raw film stock.

Edison employed "goon squads" — enforcers who traveled to independent production locations, confiscated cameras, destroyed equipment, and in some cases assaulted crew members. Independent producers began moving their operations as far from New York as geography allowed — first to Florida, then to Cuba, and eventually to the American West.

Southern California — nearly 3,000 miles from Edison's New Jersey base — offered:

  • Maximum geographic distance from Trust enforcement agents
  • A quick escape route to Mexico if investigators appeared on set
  • Large open spaces with no neighbors to report suspicious activity
  • Reliable weather for year-round production — maximizing output between raids

The western migration was not, at first, about sunlight or scenery. It was about survival.

Part IV-B

The Moguls — Who Actually Built Hollywood

1908 – 1920s

The Men

The independent producers who fled the Edison Trust and built Hollywood were not established American businessmen. They were Eastern European Jewish immigrants locked out of established industries by institutional anti-Semitism. Banking, law, academia, and the established press were largely closed to them. The film business, considered low-status and disreputable, was one of the few entrepreneurial spaces where outsiders could compete.

The Edison Trust itself was dominated by old-money Protestant establishment figures. The independents fleeing west were escaping two monopolies simultaneously — Edison's legal and physical stranglehold on film technology, and the broader social exclusion that kept Jewish entrepreneurs out of more respectable industries. Hollywood became, in the words of historian Neal Gabler, an industry built by outsiders, for outsiders.

Among those who made the journey west: Jesse Lasky, Samuel Goldfish (later Goldwyn), Adolph Zukor, Louis B. Mayer, and William Fox.

1913 · Flagstaff

By late 1913, Los Angeles was already an established film city: Edendale had been operating for four years. When Jesse Lasky and Samuel Goldfish sent director Cecil B. DeMille west on December 12, 1913, Los Angeles was the understood destination.

They tried Flagstaff, Arizona first. DeMille stepped off the train, decided it was wrong for the picture, and boarded again for Los Angeles. He arrived knowing about Edendale — and consciously rejected it. The hills were wrong for a western epic. The colony was crowded. There was no room to build at the scale a feature demanded.

At the Alexandria Hotel in downtown Los Angeles, an actor friend tipped him to an available barn in a quiet agricultural suburb a few miles west. DeMille drove out, and on December 22, 1913 signed a lease for $250 a month. The suburb was called Hollywood.

"Flagstaff no good for our purposes. Have rented a barn in a place called Hollywood."

Cecil B. DeMille · Telegram to Jesse Lasky & Samuel Goldfish · December 1913
Dec 22, 1913 · The Barn

The barn at Selma Avenue and Vine Street was flat, open, and three miles from Edendale's crowded canyon colony. DeMille shot The Squaw Man there, completing it on February 10, 1914 — the first feature-length film made in Hollywood. The barn was the seed of what is now Paramount Pictures.

The significance is not that Hollywood was discovered. It is that Edendale was rejected — deliberately, by a man who knew exactly what he was doing — because by 1913 it no longer had room to grow.

1915 · Freedom

The United States Supreme Court ruled that the Motion Picture Patents Company constituted an illegal monopoly and ordered its dissolution. The men who had fled east to west were now free to build openly, legally, and at scale:

  • Universal Pictures — Carl Laemmle, who had fought the Trust openly for years
  • Paramount Pictures — grown from the Lasky-DeMille barn at Selma & Vine
  • 20th Century Fox — William Fox, a Trust target since 1908
  • MGM — built in part by Louis B. Mayer, who had operated underground in New England
Source Note
  • The "goon squads," Cuba refuge, and Flagstaff telegram details derive from the Lucasfilm documentary The Rise of the Moguls: The Men Who Built Hollywood.
  • The Jewish immigrant entrepreneurial framing draws on Neal Gabler, An Empire of Their Own: How the Jews Invented Hollywood (Crown, 1988).
  • The Flagstaff telegram is widely attributed to DeMille in industry histories, though exact wording varies slightly across sources.
Part V

The Competition Years

1908 – 1915

1908 – 1915

During this period the American film industry remains geographically fluid. Competing production centers include Jacksonville, San Antonio, New Orleans, and Phoenix. Jacksonville becomes the most significant rival, with roughly 25–30 studios operating there seasonally — sometimes described as the "Winter Film Capital."

However, Florida production faces structural limits: seasonal operations, limited terrain diversity, and civic resistance to film crews.

Part VI

Industrial Lock-In

1912 – 1915

1912 – 1915

Between 1912 and 1915 the industry consolidates in Southern California. Reinforcing forces include accelerating studio construction, clustering of labor and suppliers, real-estate development supporting studios, and increasing demand for consistent outdoor locations for feature films. Once this agglomeration economy forms, relocation becomes increasingly difficult. By 1915, Los Angeles becomes the dominant American film-production center.

Key Insights
  • Edendale's hills created early cinema's visual language — chase comedies, canyon scenics, winding roads — but those same hills became physical walls against expansion once the feature-film format demanded flat acreage. Edendale gave the movies their look; Hollywood gave them their scale.
  • The transition crystallizes on October 27, 1911 — Francis Boggs killed at Edendale; Nestor Studios opens in Hollywood the same afternoon.
  • The Selig 1907 catalog reveals the company had dispatched cameramen to Los Angeles for actuality subjects before Boggs's narrative assignment — making Selig's California presence a deliberate organizational practice.
  • The westward migration was driven first by physical survival — escaping Edison's goon squads — and only secondarily by climate and scenery.
  • The founding moguls were overwhelmingly Jewish immigrant entrepreneurs escaping two monopolies at once: Edison's patent stranglehold and East Coast social exclusion.
  • DeMille's 1913 Flagstaff telegram is often told as an accident — but LA was already the obvious destination, with Nestor, Edendale, and Griffith all operating there before he arrived.
  • Hollywood's eventual dominance resulted from climate, distance from Edison, terrain geometry, industrial clustering, and the 1915 Supreme Court dissolution of the Trust.
