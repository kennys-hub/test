Overview 📝

This technical report provides a comprehensive analysis of the Camino Rio arroyo adjacent to the Canyon del Rio property in Valencia County, NM, located at coordinates 34.6911, -106.5575. The report evaluates the arroyo's characteristics, its classification, and its status under the Waters of the United States (WOTUS) framework as of April 2025. It also examines the arroyo's role within the regional hydrological network and its compliance with local and federal regulations.

The Camino Rio arroyo is 20–30 ft southeast of the lot’s southern boundary and 5–10 ft below, running diagonally southwest to northeast. It is ephemeral, dry 99.9% of the time, with rare runoff (~9–10 inches/year), and connects to the Rio Grande (10–15 miles away) only during extreme events such as 100-year storms. The property’s eco-friendly design ensures minimal environmental impact, supporting a low WOTUS likelihood.

WOTUS Assessment ⚖️

Regulatory Framework

The Waters of the United States (WOTUS) designation determines federal jurisdiction over water bodies under the Clean Water Act (CWA). The definition of WOTUS has evolved significantly:

  • 📜 2015 Obama-era Rule: Broadly included streams with a "significant nexus" to navigable waters, potentially covering ephemeral streams like Camino Rio.
  • 📜 2020 Trump-era Navigable Waters Protection Rule: Narrowed WOTUS to traditional navigable waters, their tributaries, and adjacent wetlands, often excluding ephemeral streams.
  • 📜 2023 Sackett v. EPA Decision: Limited WOTUS to waters with a "continuous surface connection" to navigable waters, requiring direct abutting. This excluded most ephemeral streams unless they directly connect to navigable waters.
  • 📜 March 2025 EPA Guidance: Clarified "continuous surface connection" as "abutting (or touching)," reinforcing the exclusion of intermittent and ephemeral streams without direct, unbroken connections to navigable waters.

Camino Rio WOTUS Evaluation

The Camino Rio arroyo does not meet the current WOTUS criteria:

  • 🌊 Flow Characteristics: Ephemeral, dry 99.9% of the time, with flow only during extreme events (e.g., 100-year storms). A flood depth map indicates potential depths of up to 3+ ft in the arroyo channel during such events, confirming the infrequency of flow.
  • 📏 Connection to Navigable Waters: Connects to the Rio Grande (10–15 miles away) via regional arroyos, but only during rare events. Runoff often disperses into the desert before reaching the Rio Grande, as shown in regional maps.
  • ⚖️ Continuous Surface Connection: Lacks a direct, continuous surface connection to the Rio Grande, failing the *Sackett* standard of abutting a navigable water. The National Wetlands Inventory (NWI) map confirms the absence of adjacent wetlands that might trigger WOTUS oversight.
  • 🛡️ Environmental Impact Mitigation: The property’s advanced septic system (BioMicrobics MicroFAST ATU, 115–125 ft from the arroyo), 5,000-gallon cistern, swales, basins, and vegetative buffers (prickly pear, cholla) minimize runoff, reducing potential federal oversight. The flood depth map shows minimal flooding (0–0.5 ft) on the property itself, supporting the effectiveness of these measures.

Conclusion: Camino Rio is unlikely to be classified as WOTUS under the 2023 *Sackett* ruling and March 2025 guidance, supported by the NWI classification (R4SBJ), regional hydrological maps, and flood depth data. A jurisdictional determination from the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers would provide final confirmation.

Arroyo Specifications 📏

  • 📍 Location: 20–30 ft southeast of the lot’s southern boundary (25 ft in diagram), 5–10 ft below the lot’s SE corner (5390 ft elevation).
  • 📐 Orientation: Runs diagonally southwest to northeast across the property’s southern boundary, as depicted in hydrological maps.
  • 🌊 Flow: Ephemeral, dry 99.9% of the time, with annual runoff of ~9–10 inches (approximately 0.75–0.83 ft/year). During extreme events, flood depths in the arroyo channel can reach up to 3+ ft, as shown in the flood depth map.
  • 📏 Dimensions: Channel width approximately 10–15 ft, depth 2–4 ft (based on typical arroyo morphology in the region and consistent with satellite imagery), capable of handling significant runoff during rare events.
  • 🪨 Substrate: Sandy loam with gravel, consistent with the lot’s well-draining soil, supporting minimal water retention.
  • 🌵 Vegetation: Sparse, with prickly pear and cholla along the banks, providing natural erosion control, as seen in satellite views.
  • 📈 Topography Impact: The lot’s 3.33% slope (NW at 5400 ft to SE at 5390 ft) directs runoff toward the arroyo, managed by swales, basins, and a cistern to prevent erosion. Regional elevations range from 5,074 ft near Cañada Ancha to 5,667 ft near Cañon del Trigo, indicating a downward slope toward the Rio Grande.

Arroyo Classification 🗂️

The Camino Rio arroyo is classified by the National Wetlands Inventory (NWI) as:

  • 📜 Code: R4SBJ
  • 📜 Breakdown:
    • R: Riverine system (waters within channels).
    • 4: Intermittent flow (flows only part of the year).
    • SB: Streambed (channel bottom).
    • J: Intermittently flooded (water present only during certain times).
  • 🌊 Implication: Confirms the arroyo’s ephemeral nature, with no wetland characteristics (e.g., standing water or saturated soils) that might trigger additional regulatory oversight, as verified by the NWI map.

Local Regulatory Compliance

  • 📏 Setbacks: Structures are 35 ft and septic systems 100 ft from the arroyo, per NMAC § 20.7.3, due to potential Rio Grande connection during extreme events.
  • 🛡️ FEMA Zone: Zone X (minimal flood risk), supported by eco-friendly runoff controls (swales, basins). The flood depth map shows minimal flooding on the property (0–0.5 ft) during extreme events, while the arroyo channel may see depths of 3+ ft, reinforcing the low risk to the property itself.
  • 🚽 Septic Compliance: BioMicrobics MicroFAST ATU ensures effluent quality (<5 mg/L BOD/TSS, <10 mg/L total nitrogen, <1 mg/L phosphorus, no pathogens), exceeding NMED standards (20.6.4 and 20.6.2 NMAC).

Regional Arroyo Network 🌐

Camino Rio is part of a broader hydrological network in Valencia County, connecting to the Rio Grande via several regional arroyos, as depicted in detailed hydrological maps:

  • 🌊 Arroyo del Cuervo: Joins Camino Rio to the northeast, flowing toward Rio del Oro Loop.
  • 🌊 Cañon del Trigo: Converges with Camino Rio near Loma Trigo, contributing to the same drainage system.
  • 🌊 Cañada Ancha: A larger arroyo west of the property, part of the network feeding into the Rio Grande.
  • 🌊 Rio del Oro Loop (S/N): Main conduit for regional runoff, connecting to the Rio Grande 10–15 miles away.

Despite this network, the ephemeral nature of Camino Rio and its indirect, infrequent connection to the Rio Grande (only during extreme events) support its non-WOTUS status. The flood depth map illustrates that while the arroyo channel can experience significant flooding (up to 3+ ft), runoff disperses into the desert before reaching the Rio Grande, minimizing environmental impact.
