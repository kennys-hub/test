Property Description 🌵

🌟 Breakthrough: Advanced septic system and eco-friendly design ensure zero pollution risk to the Rio Grande, with low WOTUS likelihood for the arroyo! 🌟

Tucked into the high desert of Canyon del Rio, Valencia County, NM, this 0.28-acre lot (80' × 150', 12,000 sq ft) at coordinates 34.6911, -106.5575 is Kenny’s vision for sustainable off-grid living. Located in Rio del Oro, Unit 28, Lot 25, Block 27, the lot is a rectangle oriented due north, with the longer axis (150 ft) running north-south (y-axis) and the shorter axis (80 ft) running east-west (x-axis). It has a 3.33% slope from NW (5400 ft) to SE (5390 ft). The Valencia County plat has been corrected to reflect the lot’s true position.

  • 🌱 Soil: Well-draining sandy loam, ideal for construction and supporting a small garden with water-efficient irrigation.
  • 🌊 Arroyo: Camino Rio, 20–30 ft southeast of the lot’s true southern boundary (25 ft in diagram) and 5–10 ft below (5390 ft at SE), is ephemeral, dry 99.9% of the time with rare runoff (~9–10 inches/year). It runs diagonally southwest to northeast and connects to the Rio Grande (10–15 miles away) only during extreme events.
  • 📏 Topography: 10 ft elevation drop (3.33% NW-SE), directing runoff toward the SE corner and into the arroyo below.
  • 🛡️ Zoning & Safety: Zoned RR-1, FEMA Zone X (minimal flood risk). Eco-friendly runoff management includes swales, basins, cacti rows, and a 5,000-gallon cistern. A survey is recommended to confirm boundaries.

Access and Proximity 🛤️

This off-grid ecotech homestead perfectly blends seclusion with accessibility. Arroyo Zoning and WOTUS Assessment

  • 🌊 Arroyo Zoning: The Camino Rio arroyo, classified as R4SBJ by the National Wetlands Inventory, is ephemeral and does not impose additional zoning restrictions under Valencia County RR-1 beyond standard setbacks (35 ft for structures, 100 ft for septic systems per NMAC § 20.7.3). No arroyo crossing is needed for access, avoiding permits.
  • ⚖️ WOTUS Low Possibility: The arroyo’s ephemeral nature (dry 99.9% of the time) and lack of significant nexus to navigable waters (Rio Grande, 10–15 miles away) make it unlikely to be classified as a Water of the United States (WOTUS) under the Clean Water Act. It only connects during rare extreme events (e.g., 100-year storms), and its runoff disperses into the desert, minimizing regulatory oversight. This reduces permitting complexity with the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers.

Ecotech Utilities & Systems 🔋

Fully off-grid, this homestead uses solar power, advanced wastewater treatment, and water conservation to minimize environmental impact, exceeding NMED standards and protecting the Rio Grande.

  • ☀️ Solar Power: 5 kW array (x=10–30, y=115–135), producing ~20 kWh/day, powers a 2.4 kW heat pump and all systems, reducing carbon footprint.
  • 🚽 Septic System: BioMicrobics MicroFAST 0.5 ATU tank (8' × 4', x=60–68, y=110–114) with tertiary treatment and smart sensors, ~115–125 ft from the arroyo, ensuring zero pollution risk.
  • 💧 Mound Field: 10' × 10' (x=60–70, y=100–110), elevated 2–3 ft for eco-friendly effluent dispersal.
  • 🌵 Vegetative Buffer: Prickly pear and cholla (x=0–80, y=10–30) for natural filtration and erosion control.
  • 💦 Cistern (Expanded): 5,000-gallon food-grade cistern (x=30–37, y=25–32), elevated 1–2 ft, collects ~5,000–5,600 gallons/year from the 900 sq ft roof (9–10 inches rainfall). Supplies drinking water (1,000 gallons/year for 2 people), jacuzzi (1,200 gallons/year), showers (3,285 gallons/year with low-flow 1.5 gpm showerheads), and garden irrigation. Drinking water is treated with a 50-micron sediment filter, activated carbon filter, and UV sterilizer (10W, solar-powered). Non-potable uses (jacuzzi, showers) require only a 50-micron filter. A first-flush diverter discards the initial 10–20 gallons of runoff to ensure cleaner water. Overflow is directed to a dry well (x=70–80, y=20–30).
  • ♻️ Greywater System: Collects 4,325 gallons/year from showers (3,285 gallons) and laundry (1,040 gallons, 10 gallons/load, 2 loads/week). A 50-micron filter removes solids, and a 100-gallon tank stores greywater for subsurface irrigation of cacti rows (x=0–80, y=10–30) or toilet flushing (1,825 gallons/year, 1 gallon/flush, 5 flushes/day), reducing cistern demand and enhancing water efficiency.
  • 🌊 Swales & Basins: 80' × 5' swale (x=0–80, y=5–10), primary basin (x=60–70, y=10–20), and secondary basin (x=20–30, y=10–20) manage runoff sustainably.
  • 🌱 Garden: 10' × 10' (x=30–40, y=85–95), irrigated with treated cistern water, supporting local flora.
  • 🚶 Gravel Path: 3' × 16' (x=43–46, y=20–36), connecting home, jacuzzi, and garden, using permeable materials.

Why This System is 100% Clean 🌟

The advanced wastewater treatment, water conservation, and runoff management ensure zero pollution risk to the Rio Grande, exceeding NMED and Valencia County standards for a sustainable, ecotech homestead.

  • 🧪 Multi-Stage Treatment: BioMicrobics MicroFAST® reduces BOD, TSS, and nitrogen, with NitrifAST® for nitrogen removal, a sand filter for fine solids, UV disinfection for pathogens, and a carbon filter for trace organics, ensuring near-zero pollutants.
  • ✅ Exceeds NMED Standards: Effluent achieves <5 mg/L BOD/TSS, <10 mg/L total nitrogen, <1 mg/L phosphorus, and no pathogens, surpassing NMED requirements (20.6.4 and 20.6.2 NMAC).
  • 🌊 Rio Grande Protection: The arroyo connects to the Rio Grande only during extreme events, but effluent reuse via drip irrigation, vegetative buffers (prickly pear, cholla), and the arroyo’s distance (20–30 ft southeast, 5–10 ft below) prevent contamination.
  • 📊 Monitoring: A sampling port ensures effluent meets standards, supporting NMED approval.
  • 🌞 Eco-Friendly Design: Solar power, water harvesting, greywater reuse, and natural runoff management (swales, cacti) minimize environmental impact, aligning with Canyon del Rio’s desert ecosystem.
