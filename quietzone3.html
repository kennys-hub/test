Sound Path Analysis - Canyon del Rio

Step 1: Establish Baseline and Sound Source

The quarry is located 1.10 km north of the plat near Los Lunas, NM, with elevations ranging from 5300 ft to 5460 ft, generating 22-33 dBA at that distance. This aligns with typical quarry noise levels of 80-100 dB at the source, attenuating with distance. Using the inverse square law, sound intensity decreases by 6 dB per doubling of distance. At 1.10 km (1100 m):

  • Assume quarry noise at source: ~90 dB (mid-range for quarry activity).
  • Distance attenuation: From 1 m to 1100 m, distance doubles ~10 times (log₂(1100) ≈ 10), yielding 6 dB × 10 = 60 dB.
  • Additional atmospheric absorption (at 1 kHz, ~0.005 dB/m in semi-arid conditions): 1100 m × 0.005 = 5.5 dB.
  • Total attenuation: 60 + 5.5 = 65.5 dB.
  • Resulting noise at 1.10 km: 90 - 65.5 = 24.5 dBA, within the range of 22-33 dBA. I’ll use 25 dBA as the baseline at the starting point (elevation ~5460 ft, with lower areas at 5300 ft).

Segment 1: Descent into Lull and Arroyo (Quarry to Base of Hill 1)

Topography: The path starts at the quarry (~5460 ft, with lower areas at 5300 ft) and descends to a lull near an arroyo (~5380-5400 ft, 5-10 ft below), with a northern ridge (~5440-5460 ft) providing initial shielding.

Sound Dynamics:

  • Ridge Shielding: The northern ridge reduces noise by 5 dB (adjusted from 7 dB due to quarry elevation and lower areas reducing effective height).
  • Vegetation: Sparse vegetation (sagebrush, cholla) adds ~1 dB absorption.
  • Ground Effect: Sandy loam soil absorbs ~2 dB.

dB Calculation:

  • Starting noise: 25 dBA.
  • Reduction: 5 (ridge) + 1 (vegetation) + 2 (ground) = 8 dB.
  • Noise at lull/arroyo base: 25 - 8 = 17 dBA.

Segment 2: Ascent of Hill 1

dB Calculation:

  • Noise at base: 17 dBA.

Segment 3: Descent into Lull Over Two Arroyos

Topography: The path descends to a lull (~5380-5400 ft), crossing two arroyos.

Sound Dynamics:

  • Arroyo Absorption: Each crossing reduces noise by ~5 dB (total 10 dB).
  • Lull Effect: Minimal reduction, ~1 dB.
  • Ambient Noise: Sheltered areas align with 20-25 dBA, adjusted to 20 dBA.

dB Calculation:

  • Noise at Hill 1 peak: 10 dBA.
  • Reduction: 10 (arroyos) + 1 (lull) = 11 dB.
  • Noise after arroyos: 10 - 11 = 0 dBA (below ambient).
  • Adjusted for ambient: ~20 dBA.

Segment 5: Descent to Your Plat

Topography: The plat is at ~5320 ft. The due north view shows the quarry’s elevation (~5460 ft, with lower areas at 5300 ft), adding 1-2 dB from inversions, and wind (5-10 mph northerly) adds 1-3 dB at 05:15 AM MDT.

Sound Dynamics:

  • Berm Effect: A 6-8 ft berm reduces noise by 7 dB; a 10-12 ft berm (planned) reduces by 10-12 dB.
  • Natural Sounds: Fountain produces 15-20 dBA, leaves add 10-15 dBA, combining to ~18 dBA.
  • Environmental Adjustment: Inversions (1-2 dB) and wind (1-3 dB) increase noise by 2-4 dB.

dB Calculation:

  • Noise at Hill 2 peak: 13 dBA.
  • Environmental increase: 2-4 dB.
  • Noise at plat without berm: 13 + 2-4 = 15-17 dBA.
  • With 6-8 ft berm (7 dB): 15-17 - 7 = 8-10 dBA.
  • With 10-12 ft berm (10-12 dB): 15-17 - 10-12 = 3-7 dBA.
  • Adjusted for natural sounds: 3-10 dBA + 18 dBA = ~18 dBA.

Segment 6: Final Arroyo

Topography: The path ends near an arroyo (~5380-5400 ft).

Sound Dynamics:

  • Arroyo Absorption: ~5 dB reduction.

dB Calculation:

  • Noise at plat: 18 dBA.
  • Reduction: 5 (arroyo) = 5 dB.
  • Final noise: 18 - 5 = 13 dBA.

Summary and Conclusion

Quarry noise starts at 25 dBA, reduced to 15-17 dBA at the plat without a berm, reflecting 10 dB topographic reduction and 2-4 dB from inversions/wind at 05:15 AM MDT on May 20, 2025. A 6-8 ft berm lowers it to 8-10 dBA, while a 10-12 ft berm reduces it to 3-7 dBA, meeting the 5-20 dBA target. Natural sounds (~18 dBA) dominate, keeping the total at 18 dBA. Quarry noise alone is 8-10 dBA (6-8 ft berm) or 3-7 dBA (10-12 ft berm), a 15-22 dB reduction (32- to 158-fold decrease), quieter than a library (30 dBA). The quarry’s lower areas (5300 ft) slightly reduce shielding effectiveness. Limitations include wind/inversion effects and Hill 1’s potential shielding; LiDAR or measurements are suggested.
